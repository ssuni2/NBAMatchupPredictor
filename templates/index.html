AI Models

KNN

Score: 0.6902
Accuracy: 0.5864

KNN stands for K Nearest Neighbor. The way a KNN algorithm works, is that it looks to classify a data point based on points near it (its nearest neighbors). We chose a K value to determine how many nearest points the algorithm will use in classification. For example, if we set k to 6, it will chose the 6 closest data points and use how they are classified to predict the class of our data point. For our set of data we used GridSearchCV to find out that 6 is the best k for us, maximizing our score and accuracy.Our score is how well our trained data fit our test data. Accuracy is how good our model is at correctly classifying data points. Our KNN score is moderate, and our accuracy is relatively low. However, our KNN model was the most accurate of all the models we tested. KNN is usually thought to be rudimentary and less accurate than other machine learning models; however, with our relatively random NBA data a simple model worked the best. Some improvements to our KNN model could include eliminating features that are less correlated with our target variable, as KNN models are prone to overfitting and work best with fewer features.

Random Forests

Score: 0.8565
Accuracy: 0.5455

Random Forests are just one of many ways to get a desired result. They work by using a common algorithm that involves multiple branches and the process of elimination to get to its ultimate conclusion. For example, some of the correlations within our data were a strong positive correlation to offense and a strong negative correlation to defense. If Team A is better at offense while Team B is better at defense, then we would be quite confident that Team A would win. Random Forests fit well with our data because of our ability to experiment with our parameters. Specifically, altering the max_depth (maximum number of levels in our trees) and n_estimators (number of trees in our random forest) improved our score and accuracy. Random Forests gave the highest score out of all three models we tested. Though random forests are known to handle excess features well, some kind of feature engineering is known to reduce noise and improve model performance of random forests. Figuring out which fraction of features are the most useful can improve our Random Forests.

Neural Network

Score: 0.5604
Accuracy: 0.5318

Neural networks work somewhat like a set of neurons in a brain. Each artificial "neuron" is connected to other neurons around it through edges. The strength of each one can be changed with each iteration through its weight, and it "learns" by adjusting the strength of individual neurons until it can provide an optimal result. Despite being known for having a high prediction accuracy, our multi-layer perceptron neural network wasn't the best option for our data. This may be because our dataset isn't correlated enough to draw accurate conclusions, as basketball games tend to be volatile and random.

Confusion Matrices

How to Read a Confusion Matrix

A confusion matrix is a table that describes how well a classification model works on test data (where the true values are already known).

A confusion matrix has four quadrants:

1. True Positive (TP): This represents the case when the actual data results are positive and the model correctly predicts true.

2. True Negative (TN): This represents the case when the actual data results are negative and the model correctly predicts negative.

3. False Positive (FP):This represents the case when the actual data is negative, but the model incorrectly predicts it to be positive.

4. False Negative (FN): This represents the case when the actual data is positive, but the model incorrectly predicts it to be negative.

Results and Final Conclusions

Overall, our models' scores and accuracies are lower than desired. This is because NBA data is always changing. Also, some of variables such as net rating, are subjective. Other variables, such as pace, are basically completely uncorrelated with team wins. NBA matchups are often volatile and result in upsets that our data has difficulty predicting. While the overall accuracy could be higher, our accuracy was good given the volatility of the NBA.

What Now?

As we've discussed before, the accuracy at which we can predict which team will win in a NBA match is not as high as we would like it to be. Because of this, we wish to improve our data and recognize more correlations. In the future, we hope to improve our data by using more team data that is relevant to the issue, or simply further training our model on the statistics and matchups of previous seasons. Additionally, some of our models could be improved through feature engineering. Specifically, KNN works better with fewer, highly-correlated features. While we cannot guarantee the improvement of said data and correlations given the volatility of NBA matchups, we will continue to update our data. Here's to hoping that NBA games will be less random in the future!
